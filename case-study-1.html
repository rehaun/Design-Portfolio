Redesign Case Study

What’s included:

Context

We had a newly launched product: an educational game designed to teach school subjects through gameplay. I was tasked to improve this game and make it more engaging for children.

BEFORE

AFTER

Problem

Navigation was difficult and not seamless. It felt more like their own textbook than a cohesive, unified game.

Solution

Adapting navigation to the user’s mental model for a game and making it more visually appealing.

My Role

User Research
Visual Design

Discovery

Independent Evaluation

Due to limited access to users, I conducted a self-assessment of the system. This evaluation revealed key pain points, including a lack of intuitive flow and inconsistency in visual elements that detracted from the game’s immersive experience.

previous design: users had to select the subject first, then the chapter, and finally view the list of games.

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitor analysis to see how other educational games manage navigation and user flow.

Duolingo

Observations: Lessons are separated by level. New levels cannot be accessed without testing previous material. Each level is divided into multiple units. Each time the user opens the app, they are directed to where their next step is.

Busuu

Observations: Lessons are divided by level. Each level is broken down into various units. When users return to the app, they are navigated to the point where their next step should be taken.

Khan Academy

Observations: Games are categorized by type and educational focus. Users can access all games across categories and levels at once. Presenting too many options at once can confuse users.

Ideation

HMW

The key question I aimed to answer was:

“How might we make the navigation experience simpler and more familiar, aligning with users’ previous experiences?”

Streamlined User Flow

After staying in the question-solving space for a while, I restructured the primary navigation flow, simplifying the steps users needed to take to complete tasks.

Improvements

The new flow introduced the following key improvements over the previous version:

  • Clear Overview of Content: Users could now easily see relevant content according to their progress in a single glance, making information more accessible.
  • Seamless Navigation: Once a step was completed, the next step would appear automatically, eliminating the need for manual navigation and creating a smoother, more intuitive flow for users.

Sketching and Wireframing

I created initial sketches to visualize the improved flow and shared them with the team for feedback.

Design

Enhancing Visual Elements

To address the varying number of lessons per semester, I divided the school year into 8 segments based on teachers’ two-week curriculum plan. Additionally, I enhanced gamification by designing each segment with the mascot that I had previously added to the game (with help of a freelancer graphic designer, Mahtab Nourian).

Beezie — our mascot, crafted to boost engagement and fun

I used the mascot, Beezie, to design fun visuals and added it to key parts of the game to guide users and create a connected experience.

Final Design

Before, users could only see games from one subject and had to go through all four categories to play. Now, they can view all games from a specific learning period and only need to be in one category at a time, making navigation much easier.

Reflection

Feedback

After launching the updated design, the sales team expressed great satisfaction with the improvements. This positive feedback indicates that the changes contributed significantly to increased sales.

Takeaways

  • Involving Developers Early in the Ideation Process: I learned that it’s important to include developers and other team members early in the idea phase. Their feedback helps create better solutions and makes sure the design works well with the technical needs and project goals.
  • Cross-Team Collaboration is Key: Working closely with the sales and development teams made sure the design changes met both the users’ needs and the business goals.
  • Trusting Our Instincts and Other Evaluation Methods: I learned that when we don’t have access to users, we can trust our instincts and use other methods, like self-evaluation or expert feedback, to identify areas for improvement and guide the design.
  • The Impact of Design on Business: I realized that design changes can directly affect business results, such as increasing sales.
